**Abstract**:
This paper investigates a class of bivariate symmetric exponential functions $S(p,q) = p^q + q^p + p^q q^p$ and their algebraic modifications. Although the original function possesses perfect arithmetic symmetry, its values exhibit super-exponential explosive growth, and the process of generating prime numbers is highly random. This paper proposes a modification scheme based on the difference between "self-coupling" and "cross-coupling", constructing the difference sequence $D(p,q)$. The study reveals that $D(p,q)$ possesses an extremely elegant $2 \times 2$ matrix determinant structure. This structure not only rigorously proves that the sequence inevitably yields composite numbers for any positive integer input, but also uncovers its deep divisibility laws. Building upon this, the paper generalizes the structure to $n$-dimensional space, constructing a higher-order determinant $D_n$ based on an exponential kernel function, and explores its intrinsic connections with classical determinant theory and coupling effects in complex systems.

## 1. Introduction

In number theory and combinatorics, finding elementary formulas that can precisely generate prime numbers has always been a classic and highly challenging topic. Historically, Euler proposed the famous quadratic polynomial $f(x) = x^2 + x + 41$, which can consecutively generate 40 prime numbers [1]. However, it has long been proven in mathematics that no single-variable non-constant polynomial can output prime numbers for all integers. In 1976, Jones et al. utilized Diophantine equations to construct a massive polynomial containing 26 variables, whose positive values exactly constitute the set of all prime numbers [2]. Although this is a milestone in theory, due to its extremely high computational complexity, such formulas lack operability in practical applications.

Define the bivariate symmetric function:
$$S(p,q) = p^q + q^p + p^q q^p = (1+p^q)(1+q^p) - 1$$
where $p, q$ are prime numbers. When restricting $p, q$ to be twin primes (i.e., $|p-q|=2$), this function can generate a series of enormous integers. However, due to the superposition of exponential terms, the sequence grows extremely fast, and its primality distribution exhibits pseudo-random characteristics, lacking a predictable algebraic skeleton.

To explore the underlying algebraic structure of this function, this paper attempts to modify it by introducing subtraction operations. Our goal is not merely to slow down the numerical growth, but to extract "algebraic structure" from "arithmetic randomness", and further generalize it to higher-dimensional spaces.

## 2. Construction of the Difference Sequence $D(p,q)$ and Core Identity

### 2.1 Construction Idea
In the original function $S(p,q)$, $p^q$ and $q^p$ represent the "cross-coupling" of bases and exponents, while $p^p$ and $q^q$ represent "self-coupling". To measure the algebraic tension generated by this misalignment, we define the difference sequence $D(p,q)$ (assuming $p < q$):
$$D(p,q) = (p^p + q^q + p^p q^q) - (p^q + q^p + p^q q^p)$$

### 2.2 Core Identity and Determinant Structure
By regrouping and factoring the above 6 terms, we can obtain an extremely elegant core identity:
$$D(p,q) = (p^p + 1)(q^q + 1) - (p^q + 1)(q^p + 1)$$

**Proof**: Expanding the right side yields $p^p q^q + p^p + q^q + 1 - (p^q q^p + p^q + q^p + 1)$. After canceling the constant term 1, it exactly equals the definition of $D(p,q)$.

Based on this identity, we can express $D(p,q)$ as the determinant of a $2 \times 2$ matrix:


$$
D(p,q) = \det \begin{pmatrix} p^p+1 & p^q+1 \\ q^p+1 & q^q+1 \end{pmatrix} (1)
$$


This discovery thoroughly transforms the originally messy exponential polynomial into a determinant structure with a rigorous linear algebra skeleton.

## 3. Deep Properties of the Difference Sequence and Proof of Compositeness

### 3.1 Elevation of Symmetry
The symmetry of the original function $S(p,q)$, where $S(p,q)=S(q,p)$, is based on the basic arithmetic commutative law. The symmetry of $D(p,q)$ is much more profound. In matrix theory, swapping two rows of a matrix changes the sign of the determinant (antisymmetry). When we swap $p$ and $q$, it is equivalent to **simultaneously swapping both the rows and columns of matrix (1)**. The two sign changes ($(-1) \times (-1) = 1$) keep the final result unchanged. This "macroscopic symmetry resulting from double antisymmetry" endows the sequence with a higher-dimensional structural aesthetic.

### 3.2 Inevitable Divisibility and Proof of Compositeness
Utilizing the properties of determinants, we can rigorously prove that $D(p,q)$ possesses a completely transparent factor structure that the original function lacks. Assume $p, q$ are unequal positive integers:

1.  **Inevitably divisible by $q-p$**: In the sense of modulo $q-p$, $q \equiv p$. At this point, the first row and the second row of the matrix are exactly the same. According to the properties of determinants, if two rows are identical, the determinant is 0. Therefore, $D(p,q) \equiv 0 \pmod{q-p}$.
2.  **Inevitably divisible by $p+1$ and $q+1$ (when $p,q$ are odd)**: In the sense of modulo $p+1$, $p \equiv -1$. Since $p, q$ are both odd, we have $p^p \equiv (-1)^p = -1$ and $p^q \equiv (-1)^q = -1$. At this point, **all elements in the first column of the matrix are 0**. Therefore, $D(p,q) \equiv 0 \pmod{p+1}$. Similarly, it can be proven to be divisible by $q+1$.

**Conclusion**: Since $D(p,q)$ is inevitably divisible by $p+1$, $q+1$, and $q-p$, and its value is far greater than these linear factors, **$D(p,q)$ is rigorously proven in its algebraic structure to be inevitably a composite number**. It transforms from the "accidental random primes" of the original sequence into "inevitable ordered composites".

### 3.3 Introduction of the Quotient Sequence $E(p,q)$
Since $D(p,q)$ carries "trivial factors" imposed by the algebraic structure, we strip them away and define the quotient sequence $E(p,q)$. For twin primes $q = p+2$, their least common multiple factor is $\frac{(p+1)(q+1)}{2}$, hence defined as:


$$
E(p,q) = \frac{2 \cdot D(p,q)}{(p+1)(q+1)}
$$


Studying the prime factor distribution of $E(p,q)$ will be an interesting direction for future number-theoretic computations.

## 4. Generalization of the Determinant to n-Dimensional Space

The $2 \times 2$ determinant structure is not accidental; it provides a universal algebraic template. We can naturally generalize this construction to $n$ variables $x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n$ (all positive integers).

### 4.1 Definition of the n-th Order Exponential Kernel Determinant
Define an $n$-th order square matrix $M_n$, whose elements are generated by the kernel function $K(x,y) = x^y + 1$:


$$
M_n = \begin{pmatrix} 
x_1^{x_1}+1 & x_1^{x_2}+1 & \cdots & x_1^{x_n}+1 \\ 
x_2^{x_1}+1 & x_2^{x_2}+1 & \cdots & x_2^{x_n}+1 \\ 
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 
x_n^{x_1}+1 & x_n^{x_2}+1 & \cdots & x_n^{x_n}+1 
\end{pmatrix}
$$


We define the $n$-dimensional difference function as:


$$
D_n(x_1, \dots, x_n) = \det(M_n) (2)
$$


Obviously, when $n=2$, $D_2(p,q)$ is exactly the $D(p,q)$ we studied earlier.

### 4.2 Connection with Classical Determinant Theory
In higher algebra and analytic number theory, matrices of the form $\det(f(x_i, y_j))$ are ubiquitous. For example, the famous Vandermonde determinant $\det(x_i^{j-1})$ and the Cauchy determinant $\det(\frac{1}{x_i+y_j})$ [3].
The $D_n$ constructed in this paper belongs to a special class of **asymmetric kernel function determinants**. Unlike the Vandermonde determinant, which relies on the increment of powers, the bases and exponents of $D_n$ are determined by the variables themselves. This "self-referential" characteristic makes $D_n$ highly nonlinear. When any two variables $x_i = x_j$, the matrix $M_n$ has identical rows, and the determinant $D_n = 0$. This means $D_n$ inevitably contains all factors of the form $(x_i - x_j)$, namely:


$$
\prod_{1 \le i < j \le n} (x_i - x_j) \quad \Big| \quad D_n(x_1, \dots, x_n)
$$


This provides a brand-new linear algebra tool for studying the factorization of multiple exponential polynomials.

### 4.3 System Theory and Physical Significance: Measuring the Tension between Self-reference and Interaction
Stepping out of the purely algebraic perspective, $D_n$ has a fascinating interpretation in complex system theory. In coupled networks and random matrix theory, the diagonal elements of a matrix usually represent the "self-reference" or self-energy of the system, while the off-diagonal elements represent the "interaction" or coupling energy between nodes [4].

In the matrix $M_n$:
*   The diagonal element $x_i^{x_i}+1$ represents the **self-evolution** of node $i$.
*   The off-diagonal element $x_i^{x_j}+1$ represents the **mutual interference** between node $i$ and node $j$.

The value of the determinant geometrically represents the oriented volume of an $n$-dimensional parallelotope. Therefore, $D_n$ is actually **measuring the energy difference and spatial distortion between "self-evolution" and "mutual interference" in an $n$-node system**. When all nodes are homogenized (all $x_i$ are equal), the system collapses and the volume is 0; when the differences between nodes increase, the "distortion" brought by the interaction becomes stronger, and the absolute value of $D_n$ becomes larger. This provides a minimalist mathematical model for understanding nonlinear coupling effects in complex systems [5].

## 5. Conclusion and Outlook

The subtraction modification of the symmetric exponential function $S(p,q)$ in this paper, although it did not achieve the original intention of "slowing down growth" in absolute numerical value, achieved a qualitative leap in mathematical structure. By introducing the determinant construction, we translated the originally messy 6-term exponential difference into the universal language of modern mathematics.

This construction not only rigorously proves that the difference sequence $D(p,q)$ inevitably yields composite numbers for any positive integer input, revealing the deep order of its divisibility by $p+1, q+1, q-p$, but also successfully generalizes it to $n$-dimensional space. The proposal of the $n$-th order exponential kernel determinant $D_n$ not only enriches classical determinant theory but also provides new mathematical tools for measuring coupling tension in complex systems.

Future research can further explore the asymptotic behavior of $D_n$ as $n \to \infty$, or study the primality distribution laws of the quotient sequence $E(p,q)$ after stripping the trivial factors under extremely large primes.
